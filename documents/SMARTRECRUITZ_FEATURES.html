1. Executive Summary

SmartRecruitz is a verified-talent infrastructure platform. It turns an unstructured candidate — a resume, an ID, a short interview — into a structured, verified, ranked profile that a hiring manager can trust and search in seconds.

The product does four things:

  • A guided chatbot onboarding that walks a candidate from "hello" to a scored profile in ~10 minutes
  • Five specialised AI agents that parse, de-duplicate, verify, interview, and score every candidate
  • A hiring-manager portal that searches the verified talent pool by JD, skills, and location
  • A readiness score + recommendation (L1 pass/fail) so recruiters review evidence, not claims

This document goes feature-by-feature through what each surface does, why it exists, who uses it, and how we know it's working. It deliberately avoids implementation detail — that lives in code.

2. Goals & Non-Goals

Goals (the bar we hold the product to)

#GoalHow we know it's working
G1Turn an applicant into a verified, structured profile with no manual data entry≥ 90% of candidates reach a scored profile from resume + ID alone
G2Cut recruiter screening time per candidate by ≥ 70%Time from "applicant" to "shortlist decision" measured per role
G3Every profile carries an objective readiness signal, not self-reported claims100% of talent-pool candidates have an L1 score + recommendation
G4Keep the pool clean — no duplicates, no unverified identities< 2% duplicate rate; 100% of pool candidates ID-verified
G5Recruiters find the right candidate by JD + skills + location in one searchMedian time-to-first-shortlist < 5 minutes

Non-Goals (what we explicitly don't do)

  • We don't run payroll, offers, or onboarding-after-hire. We stop at "verified, ranked, contactable."
  • We don't auto-reject candidates. Every score is advisory; the human makes the call.
  • We don't proctor or surveil. We verify identity once; we don't watch webcams or keystrokes.
  • We don't scrape the open web for candidates. Candidates opt in through onboarding.

3. Personas

P1 · Candidate

  • A job-seeker completing a one-time verification to enter a talent pool.
  • Wants: a fast, guided flow; fair scoring; to not re-type what's already on their resume.
  • Doesn't want: long forms, opaque rejection, exposure of weaknesses to other candidates.

P2 · Hiring Manager / Recruiter

  • Searches the verified pool against an open role. Reviews 5–50 candidates per requisition.
  • Wants: to find skill + location matches fast, with evidence (interview score, resume analysis) attached.
  • Doesn't want: unverified profiles, duplicates, or a wall of identical-looking resumes.

P3 · Talent / Program Admin

  • Owns pool quality and throughput — how many candidates verified, average readiness, domain mix.
  • Wants: a clean dashboard, the ability to approve/reject, and re-interview controls.
  • Doesn't want: a noisy pool or false "verified" badges that erode recruiter trust.

4. The Candidate Journey

Every feature ties to one linear journey. Understanding it makes the rest of the document land.

Register → Resume parse → Duplicate check → LinkedIn cross-check → ID verification → Interview (Q-gen + scoring) → Talent pool → Hiring-manager search → Approve / Reject

A candidate starts in staging (raw, unverified). They are promoted to the main talent pool only after parsing and a unique-identity check pass. Each AI agent owns one hop in this chain and writes structured data the next hop (and the recruiter) reads.

Status levels a candidate moves through: PENDING → VERIFIED → interview SCORED → L1 PASSED/FAILED → recommendation PROCEED_TO_L2 / MANUAL_REVIEW / REJECT.

5. Feature: Conversational Onboarding (Chatbot)

Why this exists

Traditional application forms are abandoned. A chat that asks one thing at a time, confirms what it parsed, and explains each step converts far better — and produces cleaner data because the candidate verifies it inline.

What it does

A multi-step chatbot guides the candidate through the entire journey in one session: identity details → resume upload → review → URLs & cross-check → ID documents → video/text interview → result. Progress is persisted, so a candidate can close the tab and resume exactly where they left off.

Who uses it

Candidates — primary. It is the only surface a candidate touches; everything else happens behind it.

Deep behaviour

  • It collects, in order: name, email, phone, target role, CTC, notice period, working status, current location, and preferred locations.
  • It confirms parsed data ("here's what I found in your resume") before proceeding — the candidate can re-upload.
  • Session state is persisted; on return, it resumes at the exact step rather than restarting.
  • Long-running agent calls (parse, verify, score) are shown as live "analyzing…" states with polling, never a frozen screen.

What success looks like

  • ≥ 80% of candidates who start onboarding reach a scored profile
  • Median completion time 8–12 minutes
  • < 5% of sessions abandoned at the resume-confirm step

Edge cases worth knowing

  • Returning candidate — email lookup restores their ref and jumps to the right step.
  • Agent call fails mid-flow — the candidate's input is saved; they see a graceful retry, not a lost session.

6. Feature: Agent 1 — Resume Parsing & Scoring

Why this exists

The resume is the densest signal a candidate gives us. Parsing it into structured skills, experience, and education — and scoring its quality — removes manual data entry and gives every downstream agent something clean to work with.

What it does

Takes the uploaded resume (PDF / DOCX / image) and extracts a normalised profile: full name, title, total experience, primary domain, skills (normalised against a taxonomy), experience history, education, and GitHub/LinkedIn URLs — plus an overall resume score with sub-scores and analytics.

Deep behaviour

  • Image/scanned PDFs are handled via vision; text PDFs via text extraction — whichever yields cleaner structure.
  • Skills are normalised ("ReactJS", "React.js" → "React") so search and matching are consistent.
  • Produces analytics the recruiter sees later: tenure stats, employment gaps, product/service/GCC/startup experience split.

What success looks like

  • ≥ 90% of resumes parse into a confirmable profile on first upload
  • Candidate confirms the parsed card without re-upload ≥ 80% of the time

Edge cases worth knowing

  • Unparseable / image-only resume — vision fallback; if still poor, candidate is asked to re-upload a cleaner file.
  • Missing URLs — the chatbot asks for GitHub/LinkedIn explicitly rather than leaving them blank.

7. Feature: Agent 2 — Duplicate Detection

Why this exists

A talent pool full of the same person registered three times is worthless to a recruiter. We catch re-registrations before a candidate is promoted to the main pool.

What it does

Before promotion, the new profile is compared against existing candidates — first a cheap database pass (fuzzy name match + email/phone), then an LLM comparison on the small set of near-matches. Returns UNIQUE, DUPLICATE, or UNCERTAIN with reasoning.

Deep behaviour

  • The DB filter narrows thousands of candidates to a handful before any LLM call — cheap and fast.
  • Only UNIQUE candidates are promoted to the main talent pool; duplicates are flagged, not silently dropped.

What success looks like

  • < 2% duplicate rate in the live pool; < 1% false-duplicate blocks

8. Feature: Agent 3 — Identity Verification

Why this exists

"Verified" has to mean something. An ID check once, at onboarding, lets a recruiter trust that the person behind the profile is real.

What it does

The candidate uploads a government ID (Aadhaar / PAN / Passport). Vision extracts the name, DOB, and document data, checks it against the claimed name, and assesses document authenticity — returning VERIFIED, FAILED, or MANUAL_REVIEW with a confidence score and flags.

Deep behaviour

  • Mandatory documents (Aadhaar, PAN) are ordered; Passport is optional.
  • Name mismatch lowers confidence and routes to MANUAL_REVIEW rather than auto-failing.

What success looks like

  • 100% of pool candidates have at least the mandatory documents verified; < 5% land in manual review

9. Feature: Agent 4 — Interview Question Generation

Why this exists

Generic question banks don't probe a specific candidate. Questions generated from this candidate's skills and experience are sharper and harder to game.

What it does

Reads the candidate's parsed profile and generates a tailored set of screening questions across categories (experience verification, technical, behavioural), each with difficulty, target skill, expected-answer points, and time estimate.

Deep behaviour

  • Questions target the candidate's actual claimed skills, so weak claims surface in the answers.
  • Each question carries an expected-answer framework that Agent 5 scores against.

10. Feature: Agent 5 — Interview Scoring & Readiness

Why this exists

A recruiter needs one honest number: is this candidate ready for a real interview? Agent 5 turns a transcript into a readiness score and a clear recommendation.

What it does

Scores the interview transcript against the expected-answer framework, producing an overall 0–100 score, an L1 status (PASSED / FAILED), a recommendation (PROCEED_TO_L2 / MANUAL_REVIEW / REJECT), per-dimension breakdown, and per-question validation.

Deep behaviour

  • Honest scoring: weak answers score 30–50, excellent 80–95; >95 is reserved for exceptional depth.
  • The result is cached on the candidate so the pool and recruiter read it instantly — no re-scoring.
  • Admins can grant a re-interview; the candidate is offered a fresh attempt.

What success looks like

  • ≥ 70% human–AI agreement on the pass/fail call when a recruiter also reviews

11. Feature: LinkedIn Consistency Cross-Check

Why this exists

Resumes are self-reported. Cross-checking against a public LinkedIn profile surfaces inflated titles or tenure before a recruiter spends time.

What it does

Fetches the candidate's LinkedIn profile and compares it to the parsed resume — returning a match score, a consistency verdict, and a list of specific discrepancies (company, duration, title mismatches). The flow continues regardless; the recruiter sees the mismatches and decides.

What success looks like

  • Discrepancies are surfaced, not hidden; recruiters report them as "useful" in review

12. Feature: Hiring Manager Portal — Search, Filters & Decisions

Why this exists

All the verification in the world is useless if a recruiter can't find the right person. The portal turns the verified pool into a searchable, rankable shortlist.

What it does

Three surfaces: a dashboard (pool stats, domain breakdown, recent candidates), a talent-pool browser (filter by domain, score, experience, company type, skills, location), and a JD search (paste a JD or skills → ranked candidates by match). A candidate detail page shows the full profile, resume analysis, interview results, and cross-check. The admin can approve / reject from here.

Deep behaviour

  • JD text is auto-parsed into mandatory vs preferred skills; mandatory is a hard filter, preferred boosts ranking.
  • Match score is a transparent weighted blend (mandatory 70% / preferred 30%), renormalised to 0–100.
  • Each result card carries evidence: readiness score, L1 status, resume score, skills, company-type, location.

What success looks like

  • Median time-to-first-shortlist < 5 minutes
  • ≥ 70% human–AI agreement on the top-ranked candidate's relevance

13. Feature: Location Capture & Location Search

Why this exists

Location is one of the first filters a recruiter applies and one of the most common reasons a strong candidate is the wrong fit. We capture it once and make it searchable.

What it does

During onboarding the chatbot asks the candidate's current location (and, separately, the locations they're open to working in). In the hiring-manager JD search, a Location filter matches a typed place against the candidate's current OR preferred location (case-insensitive), as a hard filter layered on top of skill ranking.

Deep behaviour

  • Two distinct fields are stored: location (where they are) and preferred_location (where they'll work).
  • A search for "Bangalore" matches a candidate based in Gurugram who is open to Bangalore — via preferred location.
  • Location can be used on its own (location-only search) or combined with skills/JD.

What success looks like

  • Location filter used in ≥ 50% of searches; zero false matches on exact-city queries

14. Architecture & Deployment

  • Backend: FastAPI (Python), async, with five AI agents orchestrated through a service → repository layering. Background work is tracked as polled tasks.
  • Frontend: Next.js + React + Tailwind, two surfaces — the candidate onboarding chatbot and the hiring-manager portal.
  • AI: OpenAI models for parsing, vision verification, question generation, and scoring.
  • Data: PostgreSQL. SmartRecruitz tables share the platform database under the application schema with an lms_recruit_ prefix, alongside the LMS tables.
  • Deployment: runs as a standalone app on its own domain with its own login; the parent LMS links across to it.

15. Cross-feature loops

The product is more than the sum of its agents because each one feeds the next.

Resume parse → fills skills/experience → Question generation targets those exact skills → Interview scoring tests the claims → Readiness score + recommendation attach to the profile → Hiring-manager search ranks on verified skills + location → Recruiter shortlists on evidence, not claims
ID verify + Duplicate check → only real, unique candidates enter the pool → every search result is trustworthy by construction

A competitor can bolt "AI resume screening" onto an ATS, but the value here is the chained verification: by the time a candidate appears in a recruiter's search, they've been parsed, de-duplicated, identity-verified, interviewed, and scored — and the recruiter sees the whole evidence trail.
